# Aurora: A New Climate-Resilient City for the Great Lakes Region

## Design Proposal for a 21st Century North American City

**Prepared**: November 2025
**Design Philosophy**: Learning from 75+ years of new city successes and failures

---

## Executive Summary

**Aurora** is a proposed new city on the southern shore of Lake Erie, strategically located between Toledo, Ohio and Detroit, Michigan. Designed as North America's first comprehensive climate-resilient city, Aurora applies hard-won lessons from global new city experiments while addressing 21st-century challenges: climate migration, freshwater security, economic transition, and social equity.

**Key Statistics**:
- **Target Population**: 150,000 (by 2050)
- **Development Timeline**: 25 years (2026-2050)
- **Core Area**: 15 square miles (initial phase)
- **Full Planning Area**: 45 square miles
- **Investment**: $18 billion (public-private partnership)
- **Primary Purpose**: Climate-resilient sustainable city, Great Lakes economic hub, freshwater innovation center

## Location Rationale

### Geographic Selection: Southern Lake Erie Shore

**Specific Site**: Between Monroe County, Michigan and Lucas County, Ohio (currently agricultural/low-density suburban)

### Why This Location?

#### 1. Climate Resilience
- **Great Lakes as climate refuge**: Freshwater abundance in water-scarce future
- **Moderate temperature**: Less extreme than coasts or interior
- **Lower wildfire risk**: Compared to western North America
- **Reduced hurricane exposure**: Compared to Atlantic/Gulf coasts
- **Climate migration destination**: Positioned for 2030-2050 population movement

#### 2. Strategic Geographic Position
- **Between two major metros**: Detroit (60 miles west), Toledo (30 miles east)
- **Existing infrastructure corridor**: I-75, freight rail, Lake Erie shipping
- **Airport access**: Detroit Metro (DTW) 50 miles, Toledo Express 25 miles
- **Great Lakes shipping**: Direct water access for freight
- **Canadian proximity**: 35 miles from Windsor, Ontario (international trade)

#### 3. Economic Opportunity
- **Rust Belt transition**: Leverage existing industrial capacity with new economy
- **Underutilized land**: Agricultural land available at reasonable cost
- **Skilled workforce**: Detroit and Toledo talent pools nearby
- **Manufacturing legacy**: Transform auto industry expertise to green technology
- **University corridor**: University of Michigan, University of Toledo, Wayne State nearby

#### 4. Existing Infrastructure
- **Power grid**: Access to regional electrical infrastructure
- **Water/wastewater**: Lake Erie water source, existing treatment capacity expandable
- **Transportation**: Highway, rail, shipping converge
- **Telecommunications**: Fiber backbone exists in corridor

#### 5. Political Feasibility
- **Interstate cooperation**: Michigan-Ohio partnership model
- **Federal interest**: Rust Belt revitalization aligns with national priorities
- **Bi-partisan appeal**: Economic development + environmental sustainability
- **Existing examples**: Similar regional cooperation in Great Lakes Compact

### Avoiding Common Mistakes

**NOT doing**:
- Building in pristine wilderness (Nusantara mistake)
- Ignoring climate realities (Jakarta, New Orleans)
- Pure greenfield with no context (Songdo, Masdar)
- Resource extraction dependency (Ordos)

**Instead**:
- Brownfield and agricultural conversion
- Climate-forward location selection
- Building on existing corridor and infrastructure
- Diversified economic base from inception

## Lessons Applied from Research

### From Successful Cities

#### Curitiba's Transit-Oriented Development
- **Apply**: BRT system as spine from day one
- **Apply**: Mixed-use development along transit corridors
- **Apply**: People-first design philosophy
- **Apply**: Continuous innovation and adaptation mindset

#### Tapiola's Environmental Integration
- **Apply**: Nature dominates, architecture secondary
- **Apply**: Preserve natural landscape features
- **Apply**: Every residence has view of natural setting
- **Apply**: Complete pedestrian-vehicle separation
- **Apply**: Nonprofit development partnership model

#### UK New Towns' Incremental Approach
- **Apply**: Build on existing settlements where present
- **Apply**: Phased development, not all at once
- **Apply**: Community input from inception
- **Apply**: Evolutionary adaptation over rigid master plan

#### Sejong City's Smart Infrastructure (selectively)
- **Apply**: Smart systems as tools, not centerpiece
- **Apply**: Data-driven operations, but human-centered design
- **Apply**: Government anchor institutions for stability

### From Failed Cities - Mistakes to Avoid

#### Brasília's Errors
- **Avoid**: Top-down imposition without community
- **Avoid**: Aesthetics prioritized over function
- **Avoid**: Car-dependent, low-density sprawl
- **Avoid**: Rigid geometric perfection

#### Songdo's Shortcomings
- **Avoid**: Technology without community
- **Avoid**: Pure corporate development
- **Avoid**: Sterile, overly controlled environment
- **Avoid**: Luxury-only positioning

#### Masdar's Mistakes
- **Avoid**: Unrealistic timelines and budgets
- **Avoid**: Single-use research park instead of mixed city
- **Avoid**: Affordability neglect
- **Avoid**: Isolated from existing communities

#### Ordos's Problems
- **Avoid**: Speculative overbuilding
- **Avoid**: Pricing out target population
- **Avoid**: Building before economic base established

## Core Design Principles

### 1. Human-Centered, Not Technology-Centered

**Principle**: Technology serves people; community creates city

**Implementation**:
- Community co-design process from inception
- Public spaces designed for spontaneous interaction
- "Smart city" systems invisible, enabling not controlling
- Human-scale architecture and streetscapes
- Cultural spaces and third places prioritized

**Learning from**: Songdo (what not to do), Tapiola (what to do)

### 2. Evolutionary Flexibility, Not Rigid Master Plan

**Principle**: Framework that accommodates organic growth and unpredictable change

**Implementation**:
- Establish infrastructure corridors and public realm
- Allow buildings and uses to evolve within framework
- Adaptive reuse encouraged
- Zoning based on form and intensity, not specific uses
- Regular plan updates based on community needs

**Learning from**: UK New Towns (success), Brasília (failure)

### 3. Mixed-Use at All Scales

**Principle**: Integrate residential, commercial, civic, cultural at neighborhood and block level

**Implementation**:
- No single-use zones
- Ground floor activation requirements
- Vertical mixed-use in buildings
- Live-work spaces encouraged
- Services within 10-minute walk
- Diverse building types and sizes

**Learning from**: Curitiba, New Urbanism principles, rejection of modernist zoning

### 4. Transit-First, Walkable, Human-Scaled

**Principle**: Design for people walking, biking, taking transit—not driving

**Implementation**:
- BRT system as primary armature
- Complete streets with protected bike lanes
- Maximum 400m (5-minute walk) to transit stop
- Pedestrian realm prioritized over vehicle realm
- Parking minimums eliminated, maximums imposed
- Car-sharing, not car-owning, as default

**Learning from**: Curitiba's BRT success, Brasília's car-dependence failure

### 5. Affordable and Socially Mixed

**Principle**: Economic diversity as strength; housing for all income levels

**Implementation**:
- Mandatory 25% affordable housing in all developments
- Maximum income ratio 5:1 in any neighborhood
- Mix of housing types: apartments, townhomes, single-family
- Community land trust for perpetual affordability
- Below-market rent tied to regional median income
- Anti-displacement protections

**Learning from**: Mixed-income research, Masdar and Songdo affordability failures

### 6. Climate-Resilient and Regenerative

**Principle**: Net-positive environmental impact; resilient to climate change

**Implementation**:
- 100% renewable energy by 2040
- Net-zero carbon from inception
- Regenerative stormwater systems (no piped drainage)
- Urban agriculture integrated throughout
- Green infrastructure prioritized over gray
- Circular economy principles
- Climate adaptation built into design

**Learning from**: Masdar's sustainability attempts, climate migration context

### 7. Economically Viable and Diversified

**Principle**: Multiple economic sectors; realistic market-based development

**Implementation**:
- Anchor institutions: university branch campus, research institute
- Five target sectors (detailed below)
- Small business incubation support
- Regional economic integration (not isolated)
- Phased to match demand
- Public-private partnership, not pure speculation

**Learning from**: Ordos speculation failure, need for economic realism

### 8. Deeply Rooted in Place

**Principle**: Integrate with Great Lakes ecology, regional culture, existing communities

**Implementation**:
- Lake Erie waterfront as public commons
- Wetland preservation and expansion
- Native species landscaping
- Great Lakes cultural institutions
- Connection to Detroit-Toledo corridor
- Respect for agricultural heritage
- Partnership with Native American tribes (historic territory)

**Learning from**: Tapiola's contextual sensitivity, Brasília's disconnection

## Spatial Framework

### Overall Structure: "Necklace of Neighborhoods"

Rather than single center or rigid grid, Aurora is structured as:

- **Lakefront Commons**: 3-mile public waterfront (no private lakefront)
- **Transit Spine**: East-west BRT corridor connecting neighborhoods
- **Five Neighborhoods**: Each 15-minute walkable, distinct character
- **Green Network**: Continuous walking/biking paths through wetlands and parks
- **Employment Clusters**: Distributed, not single CBD

### The Five Founding Neighborhoods (Phase 1-3, Years 1-15)

#### 1. **Waterfront Village** (Lakefront Commons Center)
- **Character**: Mixed-use urban center, cultural heart
- **Population**: 25,000
- **Key Features**:
  - Waterfront promenade and beach
  - Public market and food hall
  - Cultural center and performing arts venue
  - Mid-rise residential (6-12 stories)
  - Marina and water recreation center
  - Transit hub connecting all neighborhoods
- **Economic Focus**: Tourism, culture, food, retail
- **Housing Mix**: 40% affordable, emphasis on young professionals and families

#### 2. **Innovation District** (East side)
- **Character**: University campus meets startup ecosystem
- **Population**: 20,000
- **Key Features**:
  - Great Lakes Water Innovation Institute (research anchor)
  - University of Michigan-Aurora branch campus
  - Maker spaces and fabrication labs
  - Mixed-use research and residential
  - Technology incubators
- **Economic Focus**: Cleantech, water technology, advanced manufacturing
- **Housing Mix**: 30% affordable, student and researcher housing, live-work lofts

#### 3. **Greenway Neighborhood** (Interior, wetlands focus)
- **Character**: Garden city principles, nature-integrated
- **Population**: 30,000
- **Key Features**:
  - Preserved wetland corridors
  - Urban agriculture and community gardens
  - Ecological education center
  - Low-rise residential (2-4 stories, courtyard buildings)
  - Car-free pedestrian streets (inspired by Tapiola)
  - Neighborhood renewable energy microgrid
- **Economic Focus**: Urban agriculture, ecological services, education
- **Housing Mix**: 35% affordable, family-oriented, cooperative housing

#### 4. **Makers' Quarter** (West side, former industrial)
- **Character**: Adaptive reuse, artisan and small manufacturing
- **Population**: 22,000
- **Key Features**:
  - Converted warehouses and industrial buildings
  - Craft breweries, artisan food production
  - Small-scale manufacturing (green tech assembly)
  - Artist studios and galleries
  - Vocational training center
  - Freight rail siding for local manufacturing
- **Economic Focus**: Advanced manufacturing, crafts, food production, arts
- **Housing Mix**: 25% affordable, artist live-work, industrial loft conversions

#### 5. **Cooperative Commons** (South side)
- **Character**: Affordable housing innovation, community-owned
- **Population**: 28,000
- **Key Features**:
  - Community land trust headquarters
  - Cooperative housing models (co-housing, limited equity co-ops)
  - Social services hub
  - Community health center
  - Adult education and job training
  - Childcare cooperative network
  - Community-owned renewable energy
- **Economic Focus**: Social enterprise, cooperative businesses, services
- **Housing Mix**: 45% permanently affordable, worker housing, social housing

### Expansion Neighborhoods (Phase 4-5, Years 16-25)

Three additional neighborhoods planned for second phase, responding to demonstrated demand and community input.

### Green Network: The "Wetland Web"

**Structure**: Interconnected green corridors following natural drainage

**Components**:
- Preserved wetlands (40% of total area)
- Stormwater parks (bioswales, rain gardens, retention wetlands)
- Urban forest corridors
- Bike and pedestrian paths (completely separated from vehicles)
- Urban agriculture plots
- Wildlife habitat corridors
- Outdoor recreation (natural playgrounds, trails, canoe launches)

**Function**:
- Stormwater management (no piped drainage)
- Cooling and air quality
- Recreation and public health
- Biodiversity
- Food production
- Carbon sequestration
- Educational resource

**Inspiration**: Tapiola's nature-first approach + contemporary green infrastructure

## Transportation System

### Hierarchy of Mobility

1. **Walking** - Primary mode for local trips
2. **Biking** - Primary mode for intra-city trips
3. **Transit (BRT)** - Primary mode for cross-city and regional trips
4. **Shared vehicles** - For specific needs, cargo, disability access
5. **Private cars** - Accommodated but discouraged

### Bus Rapid Transit (BRT) System - "The Lakeshore Line"

**Design**: Based on Curitiba model, updated for 21st century

**Route Structure**:
- **Main Line**: East-west spine connecting all five neighborhoods
- **Waterfront Shuttle**: Along lakefront commons
- **Regional Connector**: To Detroit, Toledo, airport

**Features** (learning from Curitiba):
- Dedicated center-running lanes, fully separated
- Level boarding at tube stations with pre-payment
- 3-minute peak frequency, 7-minute off-peak
- Electric buses (zero emissions)
- Real-time arrival information
- Bike storage at all stations
- Integration with regional rail

**Transit-Oriented Development**:
- Highest density within 400m of BRT stations
- Ground-floor retail/services along BRT corridor
- Parking prohibited near stations (except bike parking)
- Mixed-use zoning along entire corridor

### Walking and Cycling Infrastructure

**Principles**:
- Complete separation from vehicles (Tapiola model)
- Dense network, not just recreational paths
- All-season design (heated key routes for snow/ice)
- Lighting and safety prioritized
- Bike highways for fast commuting
- Neighborhood greenways for casual riding

**Network**:
- Protected bike lanes on every street
- Car-free pedestrian streets in neighborhood cores
- Wetland Web trail system (80 miles total)
- Bike-share system (electric-assist bikes)
- Secure bike parking at all transit stations and buildings
- Bike repair stations throughout city

### Regional Connections

**To Detroit** (60 miles):
- BRT connection to Amtrak station (Ann Arbor/Detroit)
- Express commuter bus service
- Future: Great Lakes high-speed rail corridor

**To Toledo** (30 miles):
- BRT connection to regional transit
- Shared mobility services

**To Airport** (DTW 50 miles):
- Direct express bus service
- Future: rail connection

### Vehicle Strategy

**Cars Accommodated But Not Required**:
- Parking located at neighborhood edges (not in core)
- Parking maximums (not minimums)
- Car-share pods (electric vehicles) throughout
- Delivery and service vehicle access on all streets
- Loading zones for businesses
- Disability access prioritized

**Parking Ratios**:
- Residential: 0.5 spaces per unit maximum (vs. typical 2.0 minimum)
- Commercial: 1.0 spaces per 1000 sq ft maximum
- Shared parking encouraged
- Parking priced at market rate (not free)

**Goal**: <30% of trips by private vehicle (vs. typical North American 85%)

## Economic Strategy

### Core Economic Mission

**Transform Great Lakes industrial capacity into green economy leadership**

### Five Target Sectors (Diversification from Day One)

#### 1. Water Technology and Freshwater Innovation
**Rationale**: Great Lakes = 20% of world's freshwater; critical 21st-century resource

**Components**:
- Great Lakes Water Innovation Institute (research anchor)
- Water treatment technology companies
- Freshwater monitoring and data analytics
- Aquaculture research and production
- Water conservation technology

**Job Target**: 5,000 (mix of research, manufacturing, services)

#### 2. Advanced Green Manufacturing
**Rationale**: Leverage regional manufacturing expertise, transition to clean tech

**Components**:
- Electric vehicle component manufacturing
- Wind turbine and solar panel assembly
- Energy storage systems
- Green building materials
- Circular economy remanufacturing

**Job Target**: 8,000 (skilled manufacturing, engineering)

#### 3. Climate Resilience Services
**Rationale**: Position as North American hub for climate adaptation expertise

**Components**:
- Climate adaptation consulting
- Resilient infrastructure design
- Climate migration services
- Ecological restoration firms
- Renewable energy installation and maintenance

**Job Target**: 3,000 (professional services, skilled trades)

#### 4. Education and Research
**Rationale**: Anchor institutions provide stability and attract talent

**Components**:
- University of Michigan-Aurora (branch campus, 5,000 students)
- Great Lakes Community College
- Vocational training center
- K-12 innovation schools
- Ecological field research station

**Job Target**: 4,000 (education, research, support)

#### 5. Visitor Economy and Culture
**Rationale**: Lakefront asset, unique sustainable city attracts tourism/conferences

**Components**:
- Ecotourism and waterfront recreation
- Conference center for sustainability events
- Great Lakes cultural institutions
- Craft food and beverage sector
- Arts and cultural production

**Job Target**: 6,000 (hospitality, culture, food service)

### Small Business Ecosystem

**Support Programs**:
- Business incubator with below-market rent
- Microloan fund for local entrepreneurs
- Technical assistance and mentorship
- Preference for local businesses in procurement
- Retail kiosks and public market stalls
- Maker spaces and shared commercial kitchens

**Goal**: 60% of businesses locally owned (vs. typical 30% in new developments)

**Target**: 1.2 jobs per household (regional average ~1.1)

**Spatial Strategy**:
- Jobs distributed across all neighborhoods
- No single central business district
- Home-based business encouraged
- Live-work spaces in all neighborhoods
- Multiple employment centers

**Economic Inclusion**:
- Living wage requirement for public projects
- Local hiring preferences
- Apprenticeship programs partnering with unions
- Childcare support for working families
- Transportation subsidies for low-income workers

## Housing and Social Equity

### Housing Philosophy: "Home for Everyone"

**Core Commitment**: Aurora will never become an exclusive enclave. Economic diversity is designed in, not hoped for.

### Mandatory Affordability Framework

**25% Permanently Affordable in ALL Developments**

**Income Targets**:
- 10% of units: <30% AMI (very low income)
- 10% of units: 30-60% AMI (low income)
- 5% of units: 60-80% AMI (moderate income)
- 55% of units: Market rate
- 20% of units: Above market (luxury tier funds affordability)

**Enforcement**:
- Affordability deed restrictions (perpetual, not expiring)
- Community land trust ownership for deepest affordability
- Inclusionary zoning ordinance
- Anti-speculation tax on rapid flipping

### Diverse Housing Typology

**Mix of Housing Types** (avoiding single-family suburban monoculture):

1. **Mid-rise apartments** (6-12 stories): 40% of units
   - Mixed-income in every building
   - Ground-floor retail/services
   - Rooftop gardens and solar
   - Exemplar: Waterfront Village

2. **Low-rise multifamily** (2-4 stories): 30% of units
   - Courtyard buildings
   - Missing middle housing
   - Family-sized units
   - Exemplar: Greenway Neighborhood

3. **Townhomes and rowhouses**: 20% of units
   - Ground-related housing for families
   - Shared walls, efficient land use
   - Small private yards
   - Exemplar: Cooperative Commons

4. **Live-work units**: 5% of units
   - First floor commercial/studio, upper floor residential
   - Support local entrepreneurs
   - Activate streets
   - Exemplar: Makers' Quarter

5. **Cooperative and co-housing**: 5% of units
   - Shared facilities, private units
   - Community ownership models
   - Lower cost through sharing
   - Exemplar: Cooperative Commons

### Community Land Trust Model

**Structure**: Following successful models in Burlington VT, Portland OR

**How It Works**:
- Land owned by nonprofit community land trust
- Homes owned by residents (or long-term leases)
- Resale formulas ensure perpetual affordability
- Residents can build equity but not speculate
- Democratic governance by residents

**Target**: 35% of all housing on CLT land

### Anti-Displacement Protections

**Preventing the "Successful City = Unaffordable City" Pattern**:

- Right of first refusal for existing residents
- Rent stabilization ordinances
- Property tax abatement for long-term residents
- Community benefits agreements with developers
- Speculation tax on rapid property turnover
- Preference for existing regional residents in housing allocation

### Social Infrastructure

**Community Support Systems Built In**:

- Universal pre-K (free, high-quality childcare 0-5)
- Community health centers in each neighborhood
- Adult education and job training centers
- Senior centers and aging-in-place support
- Mental health and addiction services
- Immigrant and refugee services
- Domestic violence shelter and services
- Food security programs (subsidized groceries, food banks)

## Environmental Integration

### Overarching Goal: Net-Positive Environmental Impact

Aurora will leave the Great Lakes ecosystem BETTER than we found it, while creating resilient city for climate future.

### Energy System: 100% Renewable by 2040

**Energy Mix**:
- **Solar**: Rooftop and community solar arrays (40% of supply)
- **Wind**: Offshore Lake Erie wind farm (40% of supply)
- **Geothermal**: District heating/cooling from lake water (15% of supply)
- **Biogas**: From organic waste and wastewater treatment (5% of supply)

**Distribution**:
- Neighborhood microgrids with battery storage
- District energy systems for heating/cooling
- Smart grid with demand response
- Electric vehicle charging integrated throughout
- Net metering for excess residential solar

**Efficiency First**:
- Passive house standards for all buildings
- District energy reduces waste
- LED street lighting
- Heat recovery systems
- Maximum insulation requirements

### Water System: Regenerative Approach

**Source**: Lake Erie (abundant freshwater)

**Treatment**:
- Multi-stage natural filtration (wetlands, biofilters)
- Minimal chemical treatment needed
- Drinking water standards exceeded

**Stormwater** (No piped drainage):
- Bioswales and rain gardens on every street
- Wetland retention and infiltration
- Green roofs on all buildings
- Permeable paving
- 100% on-site stormwater management
- Zero runoff to lake

**Wastewater**:
- Constructed wetland treatment system
- Water recycled for irrigation and cooling
- Biosolids composted for agriculture
- Nutrients recovered (phosphorus, nitrogen)
- Discharge cleaner than intake

### Green Infrastructure: Nature as Infrastructure

**Wetland Preservation and Expansion**:
- 40% of city area as wetlands and natural areas
- Restore degraded agricultural lands to wetlands
- Connect to regional wetland corridors
- Waterfowl habitat (critical Great Lakes flyway)
- Public access for recreation and education

**Urban Forest**:
- Tree canopy target: 45% coverage (vs. typical 15-25%)
- Native species exclusively
- Street trees on every block
- Forest corridors connecting neighborhoods
- Fruit and nut trees in parks (food + shade)
- Target: 3 trees planted per resident

**Green Building Standards**:
- LEED Platinum minimum for public buildings
- Passive House standards for all residential
- Green roofs on all buildings >10,000 sq ft
- Native landscaping (no lawns requiring irrigation)
- Integrated solar on all south-facing roofs
- Embodied carbon limits on construction materials

### Food Systems: Regional and Local

**Urban Agriculture**:
- Community gardens in every neighborhood
- Rooftop greenhouses
- Vertical farming pilot projects
- Aquaponics and hydroponics
- Urban orchards and food forests
- 10% of food grown within city (realistic target)

**Regional Food Hub**:
- Connect to regional farms
- Food processing and distribution center
- Value-added food manufacturing (preserves, etc.)
- Public markets for local producers
- Farm-to-institution programs
- Composting facility (close nutrient loop)

### Waste: Circular Economy Principles

**Zero Waste Goal by 2045**:
- 90% diversion rate from landfill by 2035
- Organics composting (curbside collection)
- Comprehensive recycling and reuse
- Repair cafes and tool libraries
- Material exchange platforms
- Construction waste recycling
- Reuse/salvage requirements for demolition

**Industrial Ecology**:
- Waste from one process = input for another
- Eco-industrial parks
- Shared resource facilities
- Circular economy business incubation

### Climate Resilience

**Great Lakes Climate Challenges**:
- Lake level fluctuations
- Increased precipitation intensity
- Occasional extreme heat
- Ice storms and winter weather

**Adaptive Strategies**:
- Flexible waterfront design (adapts to lake levels)
- Green infrastructure handles storm intensity
- Cooling centers in extreme heat
- Resilient energy grid (microgrids maintain power)
- Redundant water/sewer systems
- Emergency preparedness integrated into design

## Governance Model

### Avoiding the Top-Down Trap

**Lesson from Research**: Top-down planning consistently fails. Community governance from inception is essential.

### Phased Governance Structure

#### Phase 1 (Years 1-5): Development Authority with Community Advisory Board

**Development Authority**:
- Interstate compact (Michigan-Ohio joint authority)
- Federal, state, and local government representation
- Professional planning and development staff
- Bonding and financing authority

**Community Advisory Board**:
- Elected by regional residents
- Early residents gain voting power as they move in
- Must approve all major decisions
- Community co-design process for all plans
- Binding recommendation power

#### Phase 2 (Years 6-15): Transition to Municipal Government

**Gradual Transition**:
- Incorporate as Michigan city when population reaches 25,000
- Elected city council and mayor
- Professional city manager
- Development authority transitions to advisory role
- Community land trust becomes independent

#### Phase 3 (Years 16+): Full Local Control

**Mature Municipal Government**:
- Standard city governance
- Strong mayor or council-manager (community choice)
- Participatory budgeting for capital projects
- Neighborhood councils with budget authority
- Community land trust permanent institution

### Community Participation Mechanisms

**Built into Structure, Not Optional**:

1. **Community Design Workshops**: For all major projects
2. **Participatory Budgeting**: Residents decide capital spending
3. **Neighborhood Councils**: Real decision-making power
4. **Online Engagement Platform**: Digital democracy tools
5. **Community Planning Cells**: Random selection citizen panels
6. **Youth Council**: Youth voice in decisions affecting them
7. **Regular Community Assemblies**: Direct democracy for major issues

**Goal**: Avoid Brasília/Songdo mistake of imposing plan; build Aurora WITH future residents, not FOR them.

### Regional Integration

**Not an Island**:
- Coordinate with Detroit and Toledo regional planning
- Participate in Great Lakes regional governance
- Interstate compact with Michigan and Ohio
- Federal partnership (HUD, DOT, EPA)
- Great Lakes Commission membership
- Multi-jurisdictional service agreements

## Phased Development Plan

### Philosophy: Build Incrementally, Adapt Continuously

**Lesson from Research**: Massive upfront investment with no demand = Ordos ghost city. Build as people come.

### 25-Year Timeline (2026-2050)

#### Phase 1: Foundation (Years 1-5, 2026-2030)

**Population**: 0 → 15,000

**Infrastructure**:
- BRT main line (basic route)
- Water/wastewater treatment plant
- First energy microgrid (solar + storage)
- Wetland restoration (initial 500 acres)
- Primary street network

**Development**:
- Waterfront Village (core area, 5,000 residents)
- Innovation District (initial buildings, 3,000 residents)
- Makers' Quarter (adaptive reuse begins, 2,000 residents)
- University of Michigan branch campus opens (500 students)
- Water Innovation Institute headquarters

**Focus**:
- Demonstrate viability
- Attract early adopters and pioneers
- Build complete neighborhood (not scattered development)
- Establish community governance
- Prove sustainable systems work

#### Phase 2: Expansion (Years 6-10, 2031-2035)

**Population**: 15,000 → 45,000

**Infrastructure**:
- BRT expanded (full five-neighborhood service)
- Second microgrid (wind farm operational)
- Wetland Web trails (50 miles complete)
- Expanded water treatment capacity
- Regional transit connections operational

**Development**:
- Greenway Neighborhood (complete, 12,000 residents)
- Cooperative Commons (initial phase, 8,000 residents)
- Waterfront Village expansion (to 12,000 residents)
- Innovation District expansion (to 8,000 residents)
- Makers' Quarter complete (to 5,000 residents)
- University enrollment grows to 2,500 students

**Focus**:
- Multiple complete neighborhoods
- Economic base diversifying
- Community governance maturing
- Transit ridership established
- Affordability mechanisms proven

#### Phase 3: Maturation (Years 11-15, 2036-2040)

**Population**: 45,000 → 85,000

**Infrastructure**:
- 100% renewable energy achieved
- Complete Wetland Web (80 miles)
- All neighborhood microgrids operational
- Regional high-speed rail connection (if built)
- Expanded BRT to serve full city

**Development**:
- All five founding neighborhoods complete
- Sixth neighborhood (community-designed) begins
- Cooperative Commons complete (to 15,000 residents)
- Innovation District mature (to 12,000 residents)
- University enrollment 5,000 students
- Conference center and cultural venues

**Focus**:
- City identity established
- Economic self-sufficiency
- Full municipal governance
- National model status
- Community-led expansion begins

#### Phase 4: Community-Led Growth (Years 16-20, 2041-2045)

**Population**: 85,000 → 120,000

**Infrastructure**:
- Respond to demonstrated needs
- Community decides priorities
- Potential: light rail, expanded renewable energy

**Development**:

## Conclusion: Why Aurora Can Succeed Where Others Failed

### Learning From History

Aurora's design applies 75+ years of lessons:

**From Brasília**: Beautiful architecture isn't enough—function, community, and accessibility matter more.

**From Chandigarh**: Top-down imposition fails—community must shape their city.

**From Ordos**: Build for real demand, not speculation—economic realism essential.

**From Songdo**: Technology serves people, not vice versa—human-centered design.

**From Masdar**: Sustainability requires more than money—mixed-use and affordability critical.

**From UK New Towns**: Incremental, evolutionary approach works better than massive master plans.

**From Curitiba**: Transit-oriented, people-first design creates livable, sustainable cities.

**From Tapiola**: Integrating nature creates beloved communities that endure.

### Why The Great Lakes, Why Now

**Climate Reality**: Freshwater is 21st-century oil. Great Lakes position is strategic advantage.

**Migration Patterns**: Climate migration will bring people to Great Lakes. Aurora is ready.

**Economic Transition**: Rust Belt has capacity, skill, infrastructure—needs new direction.

**Regional Support**: Not isolated experiment—part of Detroit-Toledo corridor revitalization.

**Political Moment**: Bipartisan support for infrastructure, climate action, economic development.

### The Aurora Model

**Replicable Principles**:
1. Climate-resilient location selection
2. Incremental phased development
3. Community governance from inception
4. Transit-oriented, walkable design
5. Mandatory affordability and mixing
6. Nature as infrastructure
7. Diversified economic base
8. Regional integration

**Adaptable to Other Contexts**:
- Different climates and geographies
- Various economic drivers
- Diverse cultural contexts
- Multiple governance models

### A City of Hope

Aurora represents hope that North American cities can:
- Address climate change proactively
- House everyone affordably
- Create community in an atomized age
- Integrate with nature sustainably
- Govern democratically and effectively
- Offer an alternative to sprawl

Not a utopian fantasy, but realistic application of proven principles to contemporary challenges.

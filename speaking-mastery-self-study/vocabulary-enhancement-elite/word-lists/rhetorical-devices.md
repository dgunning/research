# Rhetorical Devices Vocabulary

## Master the Language of Persuasion and Literary Technique

---

## Fundamental Rhetorical Appeals

### Aristotle's Triangle
- **Ethos** - credibility and character of speaker
  - "Establish **ethos** through expertise and integrity"
- **Pathos** - emotional appeal to audience
  - "Engage **pathos** through compelling stories"
- **Logos** - logical argument and reasoning
  - "Build **logos** with data and evidence"
- **Kairos** - opportune moment for persuasion
  - "Recognize **kairos** to maximize impact"

---

## Repetition Devices

### Structural Repetition
- **Anaphora** - repetition at beginning of successive clauses
  - "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields"

- **Epistrophe** - repetition at end of successive clauses
  - "...government of the people, by the people, for the people"

- **Symploce** - combination of anaphora and epistrophe
  - "When I was a child, I spoke as a child, I understood as a child, I thought as a child"

- **Epizeuxis** - immediate repetition for emphasis
  - "Never, never, never give up"

- **Diacope** - repetition with words in between
  - "Bond. James Bond."

### Pattern Repetition
- **Parallelism** - similar grammatical structure
  - "Easy to learn, hard to master"

- **Chiasmus** - reversed parallel structure
  - "Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country"

- **Antimetabole** - repetition of words in reverse order
  - "Eat to live, not live to eat"

- **Polysyndeton** - deliberate use of many conjunctions
  - "We lived and laughed and loved and left"

- **Asyndeton** - omission of conjunctions
  - "We came, we saw, we conquered"

## Comparison & Contrast

### Comparison Devices
- **Metaphor** - direct comparison without "like" or "as"
  - "Time is money"

- **Simile** - comparison using "like" or "as"
  - "Brave as a lion"

- **Analogy** - extended comparison explaining complex idea
  - "The brain is like a computer"

- **Allegory** - extended metaphor through entire narrative
  - "Animal Farm as political allegory"

- **Conceit** - extended, elaborate metaphor
  - "Love as a compass guiding the soul"

### Contrast Devices
- **Antithesis** - juxtaposition of contrasting ideas
  - "One small step for man, one giant leap for mankind"

- **Oxymoron** - contradictory terms together
  - "Deafening silence"

- **Paradox** - seemingly contradictory statement with truth
  - "Less is more"

- **Juxtaposition** - placing contrasting elements together
  - "The millionaire and the beggar"

- **Dichotomy** - division into two mutually exclusive groups
  - "You're either with us or against us"

## Emphasis & Amplification

### Intensification
- **Hyperbole** - deliberate exaggeration for effect
  - "I've told you a million times"

- **Amplification** - expanding upon idea for emphasis
  - "This is not just important—it's vital, critical, essential"

- **Climax** - arrangement in order of increasing importance
  - "He risked his career, his reputation, his life"

- **Auxesis** - gradual increase in intensity
  - "It's a disturbance, a disaster, a catastrophe"

### Understatement
- **Litotes** - deliberate understatement by negating opposite
  - "Not bad" (meaning good)

- **Meiosis** - intentional understatement for effect
  - "It's just a scratch" (for serious wound)

- **Euphemism** - mild term for harsh reality
  - "Passed away" instead of "died"

- **Dysphemism** - harsh term for mild reality
  - "Croaked" instead of "died"

## Sound & Rhythm

### Sound Devices
- **Alliteration** - repetition of initial consonant sounds
  - "Peter Piper picked"

- **Assonance** - repetition of vowel sounds
  - "Fleet feet sweep"

- **Consonance** - repetition of consonant sounds
  - "Pitter patter"

- **Onomatopoeia** - words that imitate sounds
  - "Buzz, hiss, crash"

- **Cacophony** - harsh, jarring sounds
  - "Jangle, clank, screech"

- **Euphony** - pleasant, harmonious sounds
  - "Cellar door" (famously euphonic)

### Rhythm Patterns
- **Cadence** - rhythmic flow of language
- **Meter** - structured rhythm in verse
- **Caesura** - deliberate pause in line
- **Enjambment** - continuation without pause

## Logical & Argumentative

### Logical Structure
- **Syllogism** - logical argument in three parts
  - Major premise → Minor premise → Conclusion

- **Enthymeme** - syllogism with unstated premise
  - "He must be guilty; he ran" (unstated: guilty people run)

- **Sorites** - chain of syllogisms
  - A→B, B→C, therefore A→C

### Argumentative Techniques
- **Apophasis** - mentioning by saying won't mention
  - "I won't mention his previous failures"

- **Prolepsis** - anticipating and answering objections
  - "You might say this is expensive, but..."

- **Procatalepsis** - raising objection then answering it
  - "But what about cost? Let me address that..."

- **Hypophora** - asking then answering own question
  - "What's the solution? Let me tell you..."

## Substitution & Reference

### Substitution Devices
- **Metonymy** - substituting associated term
  - "The White House announced" (for President)

- **Synecdoche** - part represents whole or vice versa
  - "All hands on deck" (hands = sailors)

- **Periphrasis** - circumlocution, indirect reference
  - "The Bard" (for Shakespeare)

- **Antonomasia** - epithet or phrase for proper name
  - "The Iron Lady" (for Margaret Thatcher)

### Reference Devices
- **Allusion** - indirect reference to known concept
  - "Don't be a Scrooge"

- **Apostrophe** - addressing absent person/thing
  - "Oh, Time, where have you gone?"

- **Personification** - giving human qualities to non-human
  - "The market panicked"

- **Prosopopoeia** - speaking as another person/thing
  - "If these walls could talk"

## Structural Devices

### Sentence Structure
- **Periodic sentence** - main point at end
  - "Despite the challenges, the risks, the doubts, we succeeded."

- **Loose sentence** - main point at beginning
  - "We succeeded, despite challenges, risks, and doubts."

- **Balanced sentence** - parallel structure for contrast
  - "He was rich in knowledge but poor in wisdom."

### Arrangement
- **Tricolon** - series of three parallel elements
  - "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"

- **Tetracolon** - series of four parallel elements
  - "He was brave, bold, brilliant, and blessed"

- **Isocolon** - parallel elements of same length
  - "Easy come, easy go"

## Emotional & Dramatic

### Emotional Appeals
- **Pathopoeia** - exclamation of emotion
  - "Oh, the humanity!"

- **Enargia** - vivid description for emotional effect
  - Detailed sensory imagery

- **Aposiopesis** - breaking off suddenly for effect
  - "If you do that again, I'll—"

- **Anacoenosis** - asking audience for opinion
  - "What would you have done?"

### Dramatic Devices
- **Dramatic irony** - audience knows what characters don't
- **Verbal irony** - saying opposite of meaning
- **Situational irony** - outcome opposite of expected
- **Cosmic irony** - fate mocks human efforts

---

## Advanced Techniques

### Complex Devices
- **Zeugma** - single word modifies two others differently
  - "He broke his vow and his mother's heart"

- **Syllepsis** - word used differently in relation to two others
  - "He caught the train and a cold"

- **Hendiadys** - two words express single idea
  - "Sound and fury" (for violent rage)

- **Catachresis** - deliberate use of wrong word
  - "The voice of your eyes"

### Sophisticated Patterns
- **Epanalepsis** - repetition at beginning and end
  - "The king is dead, long live the king"

- **Anadiplosis** - last word becomes first of next
  - "Fear leads to anger, anger leads to hate"

- **Polyptoton** - repetition of word in different forms
  - "Let me assert my firm belief that the only thing we have to fear is fear itself"

## Usage in Professional Context

### In Presentations
- Open with **anaphora** for rhythm
- Build **logos** with data
- Create **pathos** through stories
- Close with **tricolon** for memorability

### In Writing
- Use **metaphor** for complex concepts
- Apply **parallelism** for clarity
- Deploy **antithesis** for contrast
- Include **alliteration** for emphasis

### In Negotiations
- Employ **prolepsis** to address concerns
- Use **litotes** to soften claims
- Apply **kairos** for timing
- Build **ethos** through expertise

## Quick Reference: Power Combinations

### Opening Impact
**Anaphora** + **Climax** + **Pathos**
"We face challenges. We face uncertainty. We face the greatest test of our generation."

### Logical Argument
**Logos** + **Syllogism** + **Antithesis**
"If innovation drives growth, and growth ensures survival, then innovation is not optional—it's essential."

### Emotional Close
**Tricolon** + **Epistrophe** + **Amplification**
"We must act with courage, we must lead with courage, we must win with courage."

---

Remember: Rhetorical devices are tools. Master them, but deploy them purposefully, not ostentatiously. The best rhetoric feels natural while being carefully crafted.
